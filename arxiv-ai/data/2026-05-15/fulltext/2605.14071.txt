Distribution Corrected Offline Data Distillation for
Large Language Models
Yumeng Zhang, Zhengbang Yang, Yevin Nikhel Goonatilake, Zhuangdi Zhu
George Mason University, Fairfax, V A, USA
Abstract
Distilling reasoning traces from strong large language models into smaller ones is a
promising route to improve intelligence in resource-constrained settings. Existing
approaches face a fundamental trade-off: offline distillation from teacher-generated
traces provides high-quality, sample-efficient supervision but suffers from distri-
butional drift: during training, the student model conditions on teacher-generated
prefixes, whereas during inference the student autoregresses on self-generated pre-
fixes, leading to compounding errors over long reasoning trajectories. Meanwhile,
on-policy or self-distillation methods better match the student’s inference-time dis-
tribution, but require costly online sampling and often produce low-quality traces
in early training. We propose a principled offline reasoning distillation framework
that preserves the efficiency and supervision quality of offline teacher-generated
data while correcting teacher–student distribution drift. It adaptively emphasizes
teacher supervision that is better aligned with the student’s on-policy distribu-
tion. Evaluations on mathematical reasoning benchmarks of GSM8K, MATH,
MATH500, and harder held-out competition-style tasks, including AMC, AIME,
and OlympiadBench, show that our method improves reasoning accuracy over
prior offline distillation algorithms and yields more stable reasoning traces while
preserving instruction-following capabilities. Our work shows that lightweight,
distribution-correction-aware training can substantially strengthen offline reasoning
distillation without online rollouts.1
1 Introduction
Reasoning ability has emerged as a cornerstone of modern large language models (LLMs), largely
driven by scaled model architecture and advances in reinforcement learning (RL)-based post-training
optimization. This capability is particularly important in scientific and engineering domains such as
mathematics, robotics, and physics, where solving problems requires structured, multi-step reasoning
instead of short-horizon token prediction. Despite these advances, high-quality reasoning behavior is
still primarily observed in large or proprietary models. Enabling smaller models to acquire similar
reasoning ability is critical for practical deployment, especially in resource-constrained environments
where efficient and reliable intelligence is required. The ability to transfer reasoning competence
across models without significant performance degradation is crucial.
A common approach to reasoning distillation is to train a smaller student model on reasoning traces
generated by a stronger teacher model. In this paradigm, referred to asoffline learningorbehavior
cloning, training data are collected from an external model. In contrast,on-policymethods generate
data from the target model itself and update the model using policy-gradient RL techniques such as
GRPO [30] or token-level extensions [21, 42, 43, 46].
Offline distillation offers several advantages. It is typically more sample-efficient, and teacher-
generated trajectories provide higher-quality supervision, especially during early stages of training.

However, it also faces the inherent limitation ofdistribution drift: the distribution of teacher-generated
trajectories differs from the distribution induced by the student model during inference. As the student
generates intermediate tokens that deviate from teacher trajectories, it enters support regions that
are underrepresented in the training data. This distribution shift leads to compounding errors and
unreliable behavior. Existing approaches alleviate this issue through refined distillation objectives,
such as bidirectional divergence minimization [17], but these methods do not directly resolve the
distribution mismatch, where the optimization is still focused on a teacher-provided distribution.
We propose a principled method for LLM reasoning distillation that relies solely on offline, teacher-
provided data while reducing the distribution gap to on-policy training. We first design a general
reasoning-eliciting instruction that contextualizes a large teacher model to produce high-quality
reasoning traces. These traces serve as supervision for training a smaller student model. We then
introduce a distribution-corrected objective that incorporates an advantage-based weighting mecha-
nism, enabling the student to better align with trajectories it is likely to encounter during inference.
Our formulation provides an effective approach for reducing the teacher–student distributional drift
and is compatible with most offline distillation frameworks. We focus on mathematical reasoning as
a representative domain. Empirical results across multiple models and math benchmarks demonstrate
that our method consistently improves the reasoning performance of student models in both in-domain
and out-of-domain settings, outperforming existing offline distillation baselines and narrowing the
gap to that of online sampling methods without requiring online interaction.

3 Methodology: Distribution Corrected Distillation from Offline Data
In this paper, we aim to improve cross-model knowledge distillation for reasoning, where a teacher
LLM with more advanced reasoning ability provides supervision for a student LLM. To elicit strong
teacher behavior, we first design a reasoning instruction as a conditional context c when sampling
traces from the teacher: y∼π T (x;c) , which forms an offline reasoning dataset for student distillation.
Our goal is to retain the data-efficiency benefits of offline distillation while reducing the distributional
gap between teacher-generated traces and the student’s on-policy behavior. The core idea is to
introduce a student-support-aware correction that reweights a training trajectory {x, y∼π T (x;c)}

4 Related Work
We study offline LLM reasoning distillation that tackles the teacher-student distribution drift on fixed
offline training traces. This work resides at the intersection of knowledge distillation, post-training
optimization, and mathematical reasoning supervision.
Knowledge Distillation (KD).Knowledge Distillation was initially proposed for conventional AI
models, in which a compact student model learns from a stronger teacher via softened predictive
distributions or intermediate representations [2, 3, 13, 27]. This paradigm has been extended to LLMs,
where intermediate rationales beyond final answers can be distilled from a strong teacher to a smaller
student [ 14–16, 28]. Early reasoning-distillation studies such asFine-tune-CoT[ 14] andDistilling
Step-by-Step[ 15] established the value of teacher-generated rationales as supervision for smaller
models. Subsequent work has studied how the quality, format, and composition of teacher traces affect
distillation. Other studies have improved teacher-generated traces through rationale selection, filtering,
structured explanations, and math-specific data curation [ 5, 22, 23, 33, 35, 38, 44]. These works
motivated the use of high-quality offline teacher-generated reasoning traces. However, most methods
still train the student model by imitating fixed teacher trajectories under teacher-conditioned prefix
distributions. They therefore leave the training-inference distribution drift relatively underexplored:
the student is optimized under teacher prefixes during training, but autoregresses on its own prefixes
during inference [1, 9, 17, 18].
LLM Post-Training with On-policy vs. Offline Data.A closer line of work tackles this distribu-
tion shift more directly: Online RL and other self-improvement methods optimize on model-generated
trajectories over fixed teacher traces. PPO-style RLHF methods [25, 29] and recent reasoning-oriented
RL methods such as GRPO [7, 30] generate rollouts from the target model and update the model
using outcome or process rewards. Related online or mixed-policy distillation methods, including
GKD[ 1] andCoTD-PO[ 24], also use student-conditioned or iteratively generated trajectories to
better match inference-time behavior. These methods directly reduce the gap between training and
inference distributions, but they require additional online sampling, substantially more training-time
tokens, and often encounter low-quality student-generated traces early in training. A complementary
line of work focuses on redesigning the distillation directions.MiniLLM[ 9],DistiLLM[ 18], and
ToDi[ 17] modify autoregressive knowledge-distillation losses through reverse KL, skewed KL, or
token-wise divergence control, to better align training and generation behavior. Methods such as
DistiLLM still require online student sampling to minimize the reverse KL divergence. Our method
resides in the purely offline setting, but focuses on the correction at a different level: instead of
changing the divergence measure, we adaptively reweight teacher-token supervision based on the
student–teacher distribution mismatch. In this sense, our method changes how the offline teacher
supervision is allocated, thus producing a distribution-corrected training signal while preserving the
sample efficiency of fixed teacher traces. Our formulation can be readily combined with SFT and
other KL-based divergence measures. Compared with online methods such as GKD and CoTD-PO,
4

our method remains fully offline without requiring student rollouts. Prior offline distillation work,
such as ToDi, has mainly been applied to the instruction-following domain; we studied DISCORD’s
effect in mathematical reasoning distillation as an empirically more challenging domain (Sec 5.2).
Math Reasoning Supervision and Evaluation.Mathematical reasoning also motivates fine-
grained supervision: uniform imitation treats all teacher tokens equally, even though an early
algebraic or logical error can propagate through an entire solution. This has motivated process-level
supervision and verifier-style frameworks. Outcome-level verifiers and reward models judge complete
solutions or final answers [6, 31], while process-supervised methods provide finer-grained feedback
on intermediate reasoning steps [20, 34, 36]. Related correction-based frameworks further use teacher
feedback to identify and revise erroneous reasoning traces [41].
Our work is complementary to this literature: we design a chain-of-thought reasoning template to
elicit strong reasoning traces from the teacher model without introducing a new verifier or search
procedure. We then move beyond uniform token imitation and use mismatch-aware reweighting to
emphasize teacher supervision that better aligns with the student-conditioned inference distribution.
5 Experiments
We evaluate our method on mathematical reasoning benchmarks across diverse teacher-student
configurations, varying model families, sizes, and tasks, with both in- and out-of-domain assessment
and qualitative trace analysis. We provide additional results on more experimental settings in the
Appendix C. Our main experiments were organized around four questions:
Q1.Does our offline distillation method improve the student model’s reasoning compared to state-of-
the-art offline distillation counterparts (Section 5.2)?
Q2.Is the distillation benefit consistent across different model families (Section 5.3)?
Q3.Does the method actually reduce teacher-student behavior divergence under student-generated
prefixes during inference time (Section 5.4)?
Q4.Does the resulting student reasoning performance reflect higher quality in correctness, concise-
ness, and reliability (Section 5.5)?
Q5.Why do we adopt the proposed transformations for stabilizing the distribution correction weights
instead of alternative formulations (Section 5.6)?
5.1 Experimental Setup: Baselines, Metrics, and Evaluation Protocols
Models and Teacher Reasoning Instruction.We used Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-14B-
Instruct as teachers [26], with Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, and Llama-3.2-3B as
students. Teacher traces were generated offline using a structured reasoning prompt (Appendix B)
that elicits five stages: problem understanding, strategy selection, solution, verification, and final
answer. We retained only traces where the teacher’s final answer is correct for distillation training.
The same generation-and-filtering pipeline was used for both MATH and GSM8K training data.
Benchmarks.For in-domain evaluation, we distill on MATH [ 12] and GSM8K [6] training splits
and evaluate on their corresponding test sets,e.g.,using MATH500 [ 20] for MATH-trained check-
points. For out-of-domain (OOD) evaluation, models trained on MATH were further tested on
OlympiadBench [11]2, Omni-Math [8], and AMC23, which do not appear in the training domain.
Baselines.We compared both fully offline distillation objectives and an online baseline. SFT uses
the basic cross-entropy loss on teacher-generated reasoning traces.KL uses KL divergence to measure
teacher-student distribution difference on the same offline traces,symmetric KL combines forward
KL and reverse KL divergence terms, following the broader line of LLM distillation work that studies
forward/reverse KL combinations and bidirectional divergence objectives [ 17, 40]. ToDi [17] is
an offline distillation baseline. GKD [1] is anonlinemethod reported for reference that relies on
frequent sampling of student-generated traces for training. For objectives where our correction is
applicable, including SFT and KL, the DISCORD-augmented variant applies the proposed token-level
reweighting to the same fixed teacher traces. See Appendix G for detailed formulation. We also
report the base model, the CoT-prompted student, and the CoT-prompted teacher as reference points
for interpreting lower-bound, prompting-only, and teacher-level performance.

5.2 Reasoning Accuracy Comparison
This subsection evaluates final-answer accuracy along two axes: (i) whether DISCORD improves
fully offline teacher-trace distillation compared with prior art, and (ii) how offline distillation methods
compare with online training with student-rollouts such as GKD, and how they compare with
tuning-free methods using a carefully designed reasoning instruction. The results lead to three main
observations:
First, DISCORD yields the most consistent gains on MATH-style reasoning tasks, where solutions
typically require longer reasoning traces and prefix drift is more likely to affect generation. As shown
in Table 1, on the in-domain MATH500 benchmark, DISCORD improves the corresponding offline
objectives in the main Qwen settings. The gains are not limited to standard teacher-CoT SFT: our
method also improves the symmetric-KL objective in multiple settings, suggesting that the proposed
correction can serve as a flexible reweighting mechanism for different offline distillation losses.
Comparisons with tuning-free prompting further highlight this effect. In the 7B→3B setting, SFT un-
derperforms the instruction-prompted student on MATH500, while DISCORD achieves performance
beyond instruction prompting. This indicates that direct behavior cloning on teacher traces may
weaken the student’s potential for reasoning elicitation, whereas our method allocates supervision
more effectively to teacher tokens that remain compatible with the student’s own generation behavior.
Results on GSM8K are less discriminative, likely because the task requires shorter reasoning traces
and is less impacted by prefix distribution drift. Strong baselines already plateau after training on
traces generated by our reasoning instruction. For example, in the 7B→3B setting, SFT+DISCORD
is slightly below SFT on GSM8K, and symmetric KL+DISCORD is tied with symmetric KL, even
though DISCORD improves the corresponding MATH500 results and several OOD benchmarks.
We consider GSM8K as a shorter-trace setting where the benefit of distribution correction is less
pronounced. This interpretation is consistent with our ablations in Section 5.6 and Appendix A,
where different transformations of the correction weight have more pronounced effects on longer
MATH-style tasks, whereas GSM8K is less sensitive to a specific correction-weighting design.
Second, the improvements transfer to OOD competition-style benchmarks. In the 7B→3B setting,
DISCORD outperforms the corresponding offline objectives on several OOD evaluations, includ-
ing OlympiadBench, Omni-Math, and AMC23. These results suggest that distribution-corrected
distillation improves generalization to related competition-style mathematical reasoning tasks.

5.5 Reasoning Quality Analysis
In addition to the improved final-answer accuracy and reduced teacher-student distribution drift as

such as the section headers: “ PLAN”, “VERIFICATION”, etc. Specifically, in the OlympiadBench

more closely, but its verification step uses an incorrect factorization and counting argument that
leads to the wrong answer. (2) Second, in overlap-correct cases (example E–H), where both models
reach the correct final answer, DISCORD often produces a cleaner and more direct solution, with
fewer unnecessary branches and more relevant checks. These observations are consistent with
the aforementioned metrics: DISCORD can improve reasoning trace reliability by preserving core
reasoning content instead of verbatim imitating the teacher’s formatting.
Overall, our qualitative studies indicate that DISCORD improves reasoning trace reliability beyond
final-answer accuracy. On commonly correct examples, our method yields cleaner and more logically
stable traces; in failure-recovery cases, it more often preserves the mathematical structure needed to
reach the correct answer. These findings imply that student-support-aware distribution correction
strengthens offline reasoning distillation not by enforcing surface-level template imitation, but by
reallocating teacher supervision toward reasoning behaviors that the student can more reliably carry
through at inference time.

6 Conclusion and Future Work
We propose a simple yet effective offline distillation method for mitigating teacher–student distribu-
tion drift in tasks requiring long reasoning traces. Our method keeps the training data fully offline but
reweights teacher-token supervision based on a student–teacher support ratio, thereby prioritizing
teacher behaviors that are more compatible with the student’s inference-time distribution. Comprehen-
sive evaluation on mathematical reasoning benchmarks shows that our method improves final-answer
accuracy over strong offline baselines, narrows the gap to online student-rollout distillation, and yields
more logical and higher-quality reasoning traces. While our experiments focus on mathematical
reasoning, the proposed method can be extended to other long-horizon generation settings, including
code generation, scientific reasoning, and tool-use agents. Future work may include integrating our
method with off-policy or online training to better balance data efficiency and distillation efficacy.
Another future direction is to decouple teacher trace generation from distribution correction scoring:
when a trace-generating teacher is unavailable or unknown, a proxy reference model—potentially a
weaker one—or an arbitrary policy may be used to approximate the support-ratio weights. This may
preserve the core benefit of prioritizing student-reachable tokens while offering greater flexibility in
the trade-off between teacher data quality and robustness to training-inference distribution shift.

B Teacher Chain-of-Thought Prompt
We designed this teacher CoT prompt ourselves to elicit structured, self-verifying reasoning with
explicit understanding, strategy, solution, and verification stages. Following established prompt
design principles such as role specification, explicit output structure, and self-consistency checks
[37, 39], we refined the template on a held-out sample of training questions and did not tune the
prompt against the evaluation benchmarks.
<role>
You are a distinguished mathematics researcher and olympiad problem solver
with expertise across all mathematical domains, from elementary algebra to
advanced proof-based mathematics. You approach each problem with the rigor of
peer-reviewed research and the creativity of competitive mathematics.
</role>
<instructions>
You will be presented with mathematical problems of varying difficulty. Your
goal is to provide solutions with complete transparency of reasoning, catching
and correcting any errors through systematic verification. If your initial
approach does not work, try alternative methods.
For every problem, respond using EXACTLY the following sections and order:
1. <understanding>...</understanding>
2. <strategy>...</strategy>

C Training and Evaluation Audit Details
This section collects the implementation details used to make the experimental comparisons auditable.
The main comparison is objective-level: within each teacher–student–dataset setting, all offline
objectives are trained on the same retained teacher-generated trace corpus and differ only in the loss
or token weighting applied to that corpus.
Training corpus construction.For MATH-family experiments, teacher traces are generated from
the MATH training split ( 7,500 source problems). For GSM8K experiments, teacher traces are
generated from the GSM8K training split (7,473 source problems). Each source problem is prompted
once with the structured teacher prompt in Appendix B; the generated trace is then filtered by the
same final-answer equivalence judge used in evaluation. We retain only teacher-correct traces for
distillation. The retained subset, retained count, and average teacher-trace length are fixed before
student training and are shared by all offline objectives within the corresponding teacher–student
setting. Cross-family Llama experiments reuse the same Qwen2.5 teacher-trace construction pipeline
as the Qwen-student experiments.
Offline Training Protocol and hyperparameters.Within a teacher–student setting and data split,
the fully offline methods (SFT, KL, symmetric KL, ToDi, and DISCORD-augmented variants) are
trained on the same retained teacher-correct examples and use the same offline training recipe:
AdamW with learning rate 3×10 −6, weight decay 0.01, cosine decay with 5% warmup, gradient
clipping at norm 1.0, bfloat16 mixed precision, maximum sequence length 2048, random seed 42,
and effective batch size 16. For MATH-family runs, this corresponds to micro-batch size 1 with
gradient accumulation 16; for GSM8K, micro-batch size 2 with gradient accumulation 8. The number
of epochs is fixed within each matched setting and base objective, so changes in Table 1, Table 2, and
Table 3 are not attributable to different amounts of offline teacher data or different offline training-
token budgets. Hyperparameters that define an objective itself, such as the KL/ToDi temperature or
the sigmoid temperature for DISCORD, are selected per setting and base objective and are reported
in the corresponding table captions or rows when they differ.
Online Protocol and hyperparameters.GKD is reported separately because it is not a compute-
matched offline baseline. It uses student rollouts during training and follows its own on-policy recipe:
AdamW with learning rate 3×10 −6, weight decay 0.01, cosine decay with 5% warmup, gradient
clipping at norm 1.0, maximum sequence length 2048, effective batch size 16, β= 0.5 following
the original paper setting, and one training epoch in the reported runs, with the on-policy mixing
15

parameter λ specified by the corresponding run. We therefore treat GKD as an online reference rather
than as evidence about the matched offline training-token budget.
Evaluation protocol.All methods in a setting use the same evaluation prompt, greedy decoding
protocol, and maximum generation length. The generation caps are 2048 new tokens for GSM8K,
MATH500, and 4096 new tokens for AMC23, Omni-Math and OlympiadBench. Final-answer
correctness is judged by GPT-5.4-mini using the same mathematical-equivalence prompt for every
method. The judge is not given the method identity and is instructed to ignore reasoning quality
and compare only the final answer. If a solution contains multiple candidate answers, the judge
follows the explicitly marked final answer, such as one introduced by “final”, “answer”, “therefore”,
or\boxed{}. The full judge prompt appears in Appendix D.
Compute resources.Experiments were run on SLURM-managed GPU clusters. Unless otherwise
noted, training and evaluation jobs used NVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs; GPU-hours are reported as the
number of allocated GPUs multiplied by wall-clock runtime. In the main 7B→3B comparison, the
DISCORD run uses no online student-rollout tokens and requires 3.12 GPU-hours, whereas the GKD
online baseline generates about 2.86M student-rollout tokens and requires 49.56 GPU-hours. The
reported GPU-hour comparison covers training compute for the matched distillation setting; teacher-
trace generation, benchmark evaluation, and GPT-5.4-mini judge calls are one-time preprocessing or
evaluation costs shared across methods within each setting.
