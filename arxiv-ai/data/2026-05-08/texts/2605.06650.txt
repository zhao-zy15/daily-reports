Abstract
Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), due to the deter-
ministic verification, becomes a dominant paradigm for enhancing the
reasoning ability of large language models (LLMs). The community wit-
nesses the rapid change from the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), in which GRPO reduces the
complicated advantage estimation with simple estimation over grouped
positive and negative rollouts. However, we note that negative rollouts may
admit no gradation of failure severity, and the combinatorial vastness makes
penalizing a few sampled negatives unlikely to cover a meaningful reward
signal under sparse binary rewards. In this work, we propose Positive-Only
Policy Optimization (POPO), a novel RLVR framework in which learning
can occur exclusively via online positive rollouts. Specifically, POPO uti-
lizes bounded importance sampling over the positive rollout set. Thus,
no disjoint negative rollouts are used for the gradient guidance during
the policy post-training. We show that implicit negative gradients can
emerge naturally through reinforcing the positive probability via rollouts
redistribution. Next, POPO stabilizes the policy optimization through two
mechanisms. First, it applies a siamese policy network with a momentum-
based adaptation law for stabilized policy evolution. Second, we replace
the KL-divergence with a bounded similarity penalty term in the siamese
representation space. We conduct extensive experiments using publicly
available, well-established text-LLM models, e.g., the Qwen family, across
all-level mathematical benchmarks (MATH-500, AMC23, AIME 2024/2025,
and Olympiad). Our experiment demonstrates that POPO achieves perfor-
mance comparable to, or even superior to GRPO. Notably, we show that
POPO can achieve 36.67% in AIME 2025 with Qwen-Math-7B, outperform-
ing GRPO 30.00%. Our ablation and sweep studies further illustrate the
necessity and robustness of POPO components. Our results suggest a new
RLVR direction without negative rollouts.
1 Introduction
The emergence of reasoning capabilities in large language models (LLMs) through reinforce-
ment learning (RL) makes a significant advance in the capability enhancement and human
feedback (RLHF) alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022). Well-known work, such as DeepSeek-
R1 (Guo et al., 2025) and OpenAI’s o1 series (Jaech et al., 2024), demonstrates that LLMs
can surge intelligent chain-of-thought (COT) (Wei et al., 2022) reasoning ability through
RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (Lambert et al., 2024). In general, RLVR works by
giving a sampled question x and the corresponding LLM-generated response y contain-
ing reasoning steps and a final answer, a verifier provides deterministic feedback such
as R(x, y) =1[verify(y) =True] (Cobbe et al., 2021). The simplicity of the verification
mechanism, together with various RL algorithms, has achieved substantial improvements
in mathematical problem-solving and coding tasks (Le et al., 2022), where LLMs show a
strong ability of self-reflection and generating sophisticated reasoning chains.

Despite these successes, current RLVR approaches face a fundamental challenge: the treat-
ment of incorrect responses. Take the mathematical reasoning task, for example. At the
cold-start stage, the vast majority of sampled responses are incorrect, e.g., typical pass@k
rates are usually below 50% for the AMC 23 dataset, which happens due to diverse situa-
tions such as computational errors, problem misinterpretations, and inappropriate solution
strategies (see figure 1). Thus, we begin to reconsiderdo incorrect responses provide meaningful
learning signal under the sparse reward signal setting?and thus ask the question:can a policy
improve by reinforcing only from its successes?This seems counterintuitive at first glance, given
the conventions in RL, which reinforce the policy with the good reward while penalizing
it for the bad rewards. However, a few insightful observations motivate it. First, like
GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), all correct responses share the same advantage over all incorrect
ones. Thus, there is no gradation of “how good or how wrong” a response is. Second, the
space of incorrect responses is combinatorially vast (Yue et al., 2025a) for mathematical tasks,
making it unlikely that penalizing a few sampled failures meaningfully covers the failure
modes. Third, past work in contrastive self-supervised learning (SSL) demonstrates that
effective representations are feasible using only positive samples, works such as ”Bootstrap
your own latent”(BYOL) (Grill et al., 2020) and ”Exploring simple siamese representation
learning” (SimSiam) (Chen & He, 2021), achieving powerful latent representations.
Inspired by the above, we proposePositive-Only Policy Optimization (POPO), a new on-
policy optimization approach for RLVR that learns exclusively on positive reward responses.
Specifically, POPO has the following contributions:
• POPO introduces bounded importance sampling weights, which normalize over the
positive rollout set, to preferentially reinforce positive responses, while introducing
implicit negative penalties.
• POPO uses an exponential moving average (EMA) to update the siamese policy
network as an adaptive policy anchor, combining the benefits from SSL insights.
• POPO replaces KL divergence with representation-space similarity, providing a soft
bounded constraint that preserves semantic structure in the predictor space.
To evaluate the POPO algorithm, we use the DeepScaleR-Preview-Dataset for policy training.
Then, we conduct extensive test experiments using publicly available, well-established LLM
models, including the Qwen2.5 Math family, DeepSeek-R1 distilled models, and general
text-LLM models, across all-level mathematical reasoning benchmarks (MATH-500, AMC23,
AIME 2024, AIME 2025, and Olympiad). Our experiment demonstrates that POPO still
achieves comparable or even superior performance compared with GRPO and its variants.
Especially, in AIME 2025, we show that POPO can achieve 36.67% using Qwen-Math-7B,

2 Related Work
RLHF and RLVR.RLHF addresses a critical limitation that the next-token prediction objec-
tive optimized during pretraining and supervised finetuning (SFT) phases does not directly
capture the intentions of human users. RLHF aligns LLM behavior to be close to human
intent by training a reward model on human preference and optimizing the policy via
RL (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020). However, learning reward modeling is an
imperfect proxy for human intent and thus may be sensitive to the reward hacking issue
(Gao et al., 2023). Offline preference learning, which removes the reward modeling by purely
using the contrastive preference, also has widespread usage. For example, Direct Preference
Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) has been applied for aligning the chatbot output
to user intent (Tunstall et al., 2023). On the other hand, RLVR replaces the learnable reward
modeling process with deterministic verification pipelines, where correctness can be easily
assessed in domains such as mathematical reasoning and code generation (Cobbe et al.,
2021; Zelikman et al., 2022). Recent technical reports (Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025)
demonstrate that simple RLVR elicits sophisticated COT.
Policy Optimization Methods for RLVR.Following the well-established adoption in
GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), it has become a widely used approach by removing the value
network and estimating advantages through group-level reward normalization. A series
of GRPO extensions have been proposed (Wan et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al.,
2025; Yue et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2026; Xi et al., 2025; Hong et al., 2024;
Meng et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2026; Yang et al., 2026). Among them, Dr.GRPO (Liu et al.,
2025) removes per-response length and standard-deviation normalization to correct an
optimization bias that artificially inflates advantage estimation. Dynamic sAmpling Policy
Optimization (DAPO) (Yu et al., 2025) introduces decoupled clipping, dynamic sampling
that filters zero-advantage groups, and token-level policy gradients. Soft Adaptive Policy
Optimization (SAPO) (Gao et al., 2025) replaces hard clipping with a smooth temperature-
controlled gate to preserve near-on-policy learning signals while attenuating off-policy
outliers. However, all methods remain to learn from positive and negative rollouts.
Learning without Negative Samples.Learning without negative samples (or exclusively
from positive examples) undergoes a long debate in the area of contrastive representa-
tion learning (Liu et al., 2021). The early work in self-supervised learning, such as Sim-
CLR (Chen et al., 2020) and MoCo (He et al., 2020), needs to rely on explicit negative samples.
BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) changed this paradigm by demonstrating that representations can
be learned with only positive samples through an asymmetric online-target architecture.
Meanwhile, SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021) also illustrated that a Siamese network with only a
stop-gradient operation can enable meaningful latent representation learning. Recently, in
the post-training of LLM, SFT works by training the alignment with giving prompts and
expected solutions. Thus, it can be treated as a naive way of learning without negative
samples. However, SFT-based approaches only optimize a fixed cross-entropy objective,
which clearly suffers from the limited exploration abilities. Meanwhile, SFT can also lead
to distribution mismatch as the policy may evolve unboundedly, causing the strong over-
fitting on the post-training data (Liu et al., 2023). Recent work in Online SFT (Li et al.,
2025), Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning (Yuan et al., 2023), Self-Taught Reasoner (Zelikman
et al., 2022), and ReSTEM (Singh et al., 2023) shows that iterative on-policy SFT supervision
progressively improves reasoning capabilities. To this end, our POPO closes the gap by
combining the insights from above.

the combinatorial vastness made penalizing a few sampled negatives unlikely to cover a
meaningful reward signal under sparse binary rewards. We provided a concrete example of
different RL responses given the same AIME 25 mathematical question in Appendix E.
Regarding the R1-Distill-7B model in Table 1 fourth row, while our POPO still outperformed
the baseline results (POPO: 66.22 v.s. Base: 59.98 ), our POPO slightly underperformed the
GRPO across different datasets. One potential cause was that the model entropy reduced
drastically when a larger 7B model was distilled using DeepSeek-R1. This might limit the
on-policy exploration ability, while the negative results would contain more reasonable
penalty information for GRPO (and its extensions) for efficient policy optimization. One of
our future works will consider the negative rollout effects in the Distilled model to improve
the POPO’s performance (see Discussion).
To conclude, the above results provide both model-agnostic and dataset-agnostic evidence
for applying POPO to improve mathematical reasoning ability.

5 Discussion and Conclusion
We propose the POPO framework that enables effective post-training RL via only positive
rollouts. In recent RLVR research, we also note that POPO shares a similar spirit with recent
works. For example, (Cui et al., 2026) uses a contrastive head to align reasoning paths within
the semantic space. (Yang et al., 2026) applies the teacher-student architecture for reward
extrapolation in on-policy distillation. However, POPO is unique in combining innovative
components, including bounded importance sampling, adaptive EMA anchoring, and
representation-space alignment. Further, we also provide mathematical proofs beyond the
heuristic intuition. We test POPO using well-established open-source base LLM models on
public mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Our results illustrate the potential implications
of POPO in future RLVR. However, our work has a few limitations. First, we design POPO
for sparse binary rewards, yet many practical scenarios are directly involved with dense
reward signals (Setlur et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2025). We will extend our POPO by introducing
an adaptive rollout partition mechanism for better utilizing negative rollouts. Second, POPO
is validated only on text-only LLMs with purely mathematical reasoning benchmarks. We
will extend POPO to other domains such as code generation tasks (Le et al., 2022), agentic
RL (Feng et al., 2024), and multimodal reasoning (Lu et al., 2023). Third, we only test POPO
up to 7B models. We will scale it up to 32B or 70B (Yang et al., 2024) in the future.
Disclose Use of LLMs
We used LLMs, such as Claude and GPT, for initial idea brainstorming and later to check
grammar and spelling and improve the clarity of our manuscript’s language. All scientific
contents, including figures, tables, experimental design, analysis, and interpretations, are
entirely the original work of the authors.

• MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021): a 500-problem representative subset of the
MATH test set, from the MATH benchmark that OpenAI created in theirLet’s Verify

• AMC 2023 (Li et al., 2024): a 40-problem benchmark curated from the 2023 AMC
12A and AMC 12B contests, covering short-answer high-school competition mathe-
matics.
• AIME 2024 (Li et al., 2024): a 30-problem benchmark drawn from the 2024 AIME I
and AIME II exams, consisting of challenging high-school competition problems
with numerical final answers.
• AIME 2025 (Balunovi´c et al., 2025): a 30-problem benchmark drawn from the 2025
AIME I and AIME II exams; because it was released in 2025, it is commonly used as
a fresher benchmark with lower contamination risk for models whose pretraining
predates the contest.
• OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024): the English mathematics portion of Olympiad-
